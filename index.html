Compute Freedom

From local compute sovereignty to binding global AI treaty law.

The Right to Run. The Duty to Restrain.

computefreedom.com·computefreedom.org

Two legal obligations — a lower boundary, and an upper one.

Once AI crosses a defined capability threshold, developers should be legally required to offer a version runnable on personal hardware — without cloud dependency, without ongoing access fees, without surveillance. We call this the Threshold Obligation. It secures the right of the citizen to a machine that answers to them.

Once frontier development crosses into AGI, AGI plus, and ASI, no single corporation and no single state holds the democratic mandate to proceed alone. Decisions of consequence to the species require legitimation by the species — encoded in a binding international treaty, with verifiable compute and energy limits, IAEA-grade inspections, and pause mechanisms anchored in law. We call this the Treaty Obligation.

Two thresholds. One architecture. AI is too consequential to remain in the hands of a small number of unaccountable actors — neither at the point of access, nor at the point of risk.

Open source is not the same as compute sovereignty. Releasing model weights that only function at scale on hyperscaler infrastructure is a formal gesture, not a functional one. The code may be visible; the capability remains inaccessible. Whoever controls the inference infrastructure controls the knowledge — and if that knowledge becomes foundational to medicine, education, law, and governance, concentrating it in three to five private corporations is not a market outcome we should accept by default.

History does not repeat itself. It scales.

The Mechanism

Two obligations. One architecture.

The Threshold Obligation secures the lower boundary: the right of the citizen to a machine they actually control. The Treaty Obligation secures the upper one: the right of the species to a voice in decisions taken in its name. Each obligation, alone, is symbolic. Together, they are an architecture.

The Threshold Obligation

The standard response to concerns about AI concentration is: "but the models are open source." This answer mistakes visibility for access, and access for sovereignty.

When a model's weights are released under a permissive license but can only be run meaningfully on infrastructure costing hundreds of thousands of dollars, the openness is formal, not functional. Researchers can read the code. They cannot run it. Citizens can, in theory, audit it. In practice, they cannot. The source is open. The capability is not.

Compute Freedom proposes a concrete mechanism: once an AI system crosses a defined capability threshold, its developer should be legally required to offer a compressed, locally-runnable version — on consumer hardware, offline-capable, without mandatory cloud authentication.

The Threshold — Intentionally Undefined

The precise capability threshold is left open here by design. Defining it is a technical and political question that belongs in public deliberation — with input from engineers, ethicists, policymakers, and affected communities. The obligation is the principle. The threshold is the negotiation. We invite that negotiation to begin.

What we already know:

```
  • Frontier models can today be compressed to run on consumer hardware with modest capability loss — the technical barrier is not fundamental.
  • The economic incentive to prevent local deployment is structural: cloud inference is the revenue model.
  • No legal framework currently compels disclosure of whether compression is feasible.
  • The public has no mechanism to verify industry claims about what is and is not possible.
  • Whoever controls inference controls access to the most capable AI — and by extension, to the competitive advantage it confers in medicine, law, science, and finance.

The pharmaceutical parallel is instructive. Drug developers are required to disclose formulations, submit to regulatory review, and — after a period — open compounds to generics. We accept that the public has a legitimate interest in not having life-critical knowledge permanently locked inside private infrastructure. AI is becoming life-critical knowledge. The argument is the same.

This is not an argument against commercial AI development. It is an argument for a structural floor beneath it: a minimum condition of compute sovereignty that preserves the possibility of independent verification, academic research, and democratic accountability.

```

The Treaty Obligation

The lower threshold protects the citizen. The upper threshold protects the species — and we have been quiet about it for too long.

As frontier systems advance toward general human-level capability and beyond — AGI, AGI plus, and ASI — their development becomes a matter of consequence beyond any single state or any single corporation. The current trajectory has neither democratic mandate nor binding treaty. Stargate, Colossus, the multi-hundred-billion-dollar build-outs of OpenAI, Google, and xAI — all dependent on the de facto NVIDIA monopoly on frontier training silicon — proceed at competitive tempo, accountable to shareholders, not to citizens.

The physical signature of this race is no longer subtle. When an AI company announces it requires not the electrical capacity of Silicon Valley but ten new nuclear reactors, the appropriate response of any constitutional state is not admiration. It is the question: by whose authority? That this question has not been asked — has barely been articulated — is the deepest failure of contemporary political thought.

The Failure of Self-Regulation

Anthropic publicly refuses to allow its model Claude to be used for autonomous weapons or mass surveillance. The U.S. Department of Defense has responded by designating the company a "supply chain risk" — a measure that threatens its commercial existence. OpenAI and xAI have signed contracts without such restrictions. Without treaty law, voluntary safety commitments are eventually surrendered under pressure. It is a question of time, of pressure, and of competition — not of intention.

We have built such treaties before. The Non-Proliferation Treaty. The IAEA. The Biological Weapons Convention. The Montreal Protocol. None were utopian when proposed; all of them function today — imperfect, monitored, real. The technologies they govern are no more transformative, no more dual-use, no more globally consequential than frontier AI. The institutional infrastructure exists. Only the will is missing.

What the Treaty Obligation demands:

  • A binding international agreement defining the capability and infrastructural thresholds beyond which AI development requires multilateral authorization.
  • Verifiable compute and energy limits at the frontier, monitored under an inspection regime equivalent in seriousness to the IAEA.
  • Pause mechanisms anchored in treaty law, not corporate self-restraint, triggered by demonstrated capability or demonstrated risk.
  • The inclusion of every frontier-capable state, explicitly including China, whose stated willingness to engage in international AI safety dialogue has been more substantive than the Western reflex acknowledges.
  • Public, citizen-accessible reporting on frontier development, breaking the opacity of corporate self-assessment that currently passes for governance.

The case is not ours alone. The 2023 Center for AI Safety statement — placing AI risk on the same axis as pandemics and nuclear war — was signed by Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, Sam Altman, and Demis Hassabis. The signatories include the very executives currently building the frontier. Their warning is not an outside critique. It is a confession. A confession that has produced no binding constraint on their own behavior, because none has been demanded of them.

Prices will rise. Clusters will grow. The thresholds will be crossed — in months, not in decades. Whoever does not act now will have no leverage later.

The citizen below. The species above.
The unaccountable actor: neither here nor there.

Who & Why
The sequence data is open.
The understanding is not. — On the monopoly of genomics

Compute Freedom is implemented by Michael S.D. Kormann — a scientist whose work on modified mRNA was pivotal for the delivery of genetic information into living cells without triggering immune rejection,1 a biochemical principle that would later become central to the mRNA vaccines developed during the COVID-19 pandemic. The original context was basic translational science applied to rare pediatric lung diseases, including cystic fibrosis.2

What that work made visible was a structural pattern: the raw data — genetic sequences, molecular structures, published findings — was largely accessible. What was not accessible was the capacity to understand it at scale: to model protein folding across populations, to simulate therapeutic effects, to run the inference that turned sequence data into clinical knowledge. That capacity concentrated, quietly, in institutions with the infrastructure to process it.

The sequence data was open. The understanding was not.

Today, the same architecture is reproducing itself in artificial intelligence. Model weights are sometimes released. Papers are published. But inference at the frontier — the ability to actually run the most capable systems — remains tethered to centralized cloud infrastructure controlled by a small number of corporations globally. This is not openness. It is openness as theater.

The transition from genomics to AI governance is, in this light, not a pivot. It is the same problem encountered at a different scale.3 Whoever controls the infrastructure through which foundational knowledge is generated and applied controls the knowledge itself — and, through that, the trajectory of medicine, education, law, and science.

Compute Freedom is structured as a policy initiative, not a technology project. Its goal is to establish both the Threshold Obligation and the Treaty Obligation as matters of law — minimum conditions of structural compute sovereignty that no commercial model, and no voluntary corporate framework, can substitute for.
